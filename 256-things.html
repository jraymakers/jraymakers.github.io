0. How to count.

Sorkin has no item 0. He starts at 1, as most people would.

In many cases, software engineers start counting at 0. Why? Because they’re counting units of distance from a starting point.

For example, in American football, the field starts at the 0 yard line (or goal line), not the 1 yard line. Similarly, the first memory slot starting from a given point is 0 units away, while the second location is 1 unit away.

1. The feel of a clean compile after the last fix.

Sorkin: “The feel of cool marble under bare feet.”

Many programming languages must be compiled from human-written source code into a runnable program. If the code has a syntax mistake, the compiler will produce errors instead. Fixing these is a common programming task.

A clean compile is one that produces no errors. In addition to a runnable program, it often produces a pleasant feeling of satisfaction.

2. How to work on a small team of five engineers for six months.

Sorkin: “How to live in a small room with five strangers for six months.”

Many organizations group software engineers into small teams, often called scrum teams, that typically have between 3 to 9 people. These teams, which usually last for many months before being reorganized, work towards a common purpose, exchanging tasks between people as needed.

3. With the same engineers in ship room for one week.

Sorkin: “With the same strangers in a lifeboat for one week.”

When a large project nears release, some software development organizations hold a series of daily meetings, called ship room, to track relevant work until completion. It can be a stressful situation for everyone involved.

4. The size of the stack.

Sorkin: “The modulus of rupture.”

A very common technique to keep track of running programs uses a special memory structure called a stack. In most environments, the amount of memory available for the stack is fixed and relatively small. When a program exceeds this limit, it stops running with an error known as a stack overflow.

5. The bandwidth of fiber optic cable in a data center.

Sorkin: “The distance a shout carries in the city.”

Data centers house large numbers of networked computers. Within a data center, computers are often connected with fiber optic cable, which can carry hundreds of gigabits per second.

6. The bandwidth of coffee shop Wi-Fi.

Sorkin: “The distance of a whisper.”

Wi-Fi is a brand name for a family of wireless protocols commonly used to connect personal computing devices to other networks. Many coffee shops provide Wi-Fi access, often free for customers. Speeds vary, but are rarely more than a few dozen megabits per second, and their shared nature makes them notoriously slow and unstable.

7. Everything possible about UNIX (try not to use a GUI).

Sorkin: “Everything possible about Hatshepsut’s temple (try not to see it as ‘modernist’ avant la lettre).”

The Mortuary Temple of Hatshepsut (a rare female pharaoh) holds a special place in ancient architecture, as a bridge between megalithic predecessors such as the pyramids and later Greek and Roman styles. Constructed a thousand years before the heyday of classical Athens, it cannot be considered “modern” in any sense, yet its influence is still felt today.

The UNIX operating system, built around 1970, heavily influenced nearly every later software system. In addition to major advancements in programming languages, file systems, and other foundational components, many of which remain in widespread use, it pioneered powerful command line interaction techniques, still the preferred tools of many power users over easier-but-slower graphical user interfaces (GUIs).

8. The number of people without smartphones in New York City.

Sorkin: “The number of people with rent subsidies in New York City.”

Engineers designing with the latest technology rarely stop to consider those who can’t afford it. In New York City, grand skyscrapers rise alongside apartments whose residents couldn’t stay without government support. Wealthy young urbanites sporting the latest pocket-sized supercomputer live alongside those who can barely afford decades-old communication tools.

9. In your town (include the rich).

Sorkin: “In your town (include the rich).”

Inequality, whether in wealth or access, occurs everywhere. Even those who can afford it might not adopt the latest devices. Engineers who build tools dependent on such technology would do well to remember this.

10. The practical applications of fractals.

Sorkin: “The flowering season for azaleas.”

Fractals generate mezmerizing, self-similar patterns from simple programs. Admired by many programmers for their elegance and beauty, they are often used to render realistic plants and terrain.

11. The encapsulation properties of classes.

Sorkin: “The insulating properties of glass.”

Classes are a common technique used to separate different parts of a program from each other. Data encapsulated in a class cannot be modified by other code. This helps prevent unintentional modifications, a common source of unwanted behavior.

12. The history of their design and use.

Sorkin: “The history of its production and use.”

The concepts of object-oriented programming, including classes, were developed over many years by many people. Today, nearly every major programming languages supports classes or something similar, although they vary considerably in their details.

13. And their downsides.

Sorkin: “And of its meaning.”

Although widespread, object-oriented programming has many critics. Many software engineers avoid it in favor of other styles, such as functional programming.

250. Binary and other bases.

Sorkin: “The Golden and other ratios.”

Some architects proportion their designs according to specific ratios, especially the Golden radio, for aesthetic reasons. Many ancient structures have also been claimed to incorporate the Golden ratio.

Software engineers work with machines that count in base 2 (binary), instead of the usual base 10. So, they often need to work with numbers in base 2 or related bases, such as base 8 (octal) or base 16 (hexadecimal).

Numbers that are a power of the base are easier to work with. That's why we tend to use numbers like a hundred or a thousand frequently in base 10. So, programmers often use powers of two, such as 8, 16, 32, and 64.

Some powers of two hold special significance. Computer memory is, by convention, divided into units of 8 binary digits (or bits), called bytes. Each byte can hold one of 256 different values, commonly representing the numbers 0 through 255.

So, while Sorkin included 250 items on his list, and started with item 1, the natural range for software engineers is from 0 to 255.
